The 1st _Biennale «Strangers of Providence» reveals the individual as a stranger and explores the potential for solidarity among them. Drawing inspiration from Albert Camus' novel "The Stranger," where the protagonist Meursault, living indifferently to societal norms, is eventually labeled as a 'stranger' and faces a tragic end.

While not as extreme as Meursault, we often question societal values in moments of discomfort – waking up early for work, enduring awkward situations, or facing man-made disasters. These instances, even in familiar places, turn us into strangers.

Providence, at the heart of Rhode Island, holds a symbolic meaning of divine will governing the world and universe. Its history, rooted in the separation of church and state advocated by Roger Williams, a pastor in the Massachusetts Bay Colony, began when he was expelled for challenging the unfair seizure of land from Native Americans. Williams, aided by natives, settled in the present-day Providence.

Migrants from Britain to the New World were strangers, yet they, too, turned Native Americans into strangers by seizing their land. Williams criticized the mistreatment and slaughter of natives, advocating peaceful coexistence. He proposed solidarity between strangers.

In the novel, Meursault's choices lead to complete societal alienation. Like Williams, we suggest overcoming such limits through solidarity. The unfamiliar sensations we experience individually become common ground connecting us.

In the exhibition, We would like to explore experiment practice by linking elements of each artist’s works (e.g., mind, voice, medium, theme, inspiration) to understand and empathize with different perspectives, aiming to discover the power of solidarity beyond the isolated feeling of being strangers.
